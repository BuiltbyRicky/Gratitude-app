The 5 Breathing Exercises That Actually Reduce Stress

8 min read · Updated April 2026

There are hundreds of breathing techniques out there. Most of them are variations on the same underlying mechanisms, and the evidence behind many of them is thin. This article covers only the five that have solid clinical research, are practical to do anywhere, and produce noticeable results within a single session.

The core mechanism is simple: your exhale activates your parasympathetic nervous system — the "rest and digest" system. Your inhale activates your sympathetic system — "fight or flight." Every breathing technique for stress reduction is, at its core, a way of making your exhale longer, deeper, or more complete than your inhale. The rest is detail.

1. Physiological Sigh
30 seconds
Double inhale through nose · Long exhale through mouth
Developed at Stanford by Andrew Huberman and Mark Krasnow, the physiological sigh is the fastest stress-reduction technique with a clinical evidence base. It works in one to two breaths and can be done invisibly in any situation.
  • Inhale through your nose until your lungs are about 80% full.
  • Without exhaling, take a second quick sniff through your nose to top off your lungs completely.
  • Release a long, slow exhale through your mouth until completely empty.
  • Repeat 1–3 times. You'll notice your heart rate drop almost immediately.
The double inhale pops collapsed alveoli (air sacs) in your lungs, maximizing oxygen uptake. The extended exhale then dumps CO₂ rapidly — your body's fastest stress-off switch. This is what your body does spontaneously when you sob, which is why crying often produces a feeling of release.
2. Box Breathing
5 minutes
4 counts in · 4 hold · 4 out · 4 hold
Used by Navy SEALs before high-stress operations, box breathing creates a state of calm alertness rather than sedation — making it ideal before difficult conversations, presentations, or any situation requiring both calm and sharp thinking.
  • Sit upright. Exhale all the air out of your lungs.
  • Inhale slowly through your nose for 4 counts, filling from the bottom of your lungs up.
  • Hold for 4 counts. Stay relaxed — don't clench.
  • Exhale slowly through your mouth for 4 counts, emptying completely.
  • Hold empty for 4 counts. Then repeat. Do 4–6 cycles.
The symmetrical hold phases activate the vagus nerve while keeping the nervous system in a balanced state rather than tipping fully into parasympathetic. This is why it produces calm focus rather than drowsiness.
3. 4-7-8 Breathing
5 minutes
4 in · 7 hold · 8 out
Developed by Dr. Andrew Weil, this is one of the most well-studied techniques for reducing anxiety and improving sleep onset. The extended exhale is the key — at 8 counts, it's twice as long as the inhale, maximizing parasympathetic activation.
  • Rest the tip of your tongue against the roof of your mouth behind your front teeth.
  • Exhale completely through your mouth with a whoosh sound.
  • Close your mouth and inhale through your nose for 4 counts.
  • Hold your breath for 7 counts.
  • Exhale through your mouth for 8 counts. Repeat 4 times.
The 7-count hold creates a mild hypercapnia (CO₂ buildup) that triggers a deeper, more powerful exhale. When combined with the 8-count exhale, this produces one of the strongest vagal stimulation responses of any breathing technique.
4. Diaphragmatic (Belly) Breathing
5–10 minutes
5 in · 5 out · through nose
The foundational technique. Most people breathe shallowly into the chest, which keeps the sympathetic nervous system mildly activated. Switching to diaphragmatic breathing is one of the highest-leverage long-term changes you can make to your baseline stress level.
  • Place one hand on your chest and one on your belly.
  • Inhale through your nose for 5 counts. Your belly should rise; your chest should stay relatively still.
  • Exhale through your nose for 5 counts. Feel your belly fall.
  • Continue for 5–10 minutes. This is the practice you want to return to daily.
Diaphragmatic breathing activates mechanoreceptors in the lower lungs that have direct connections to the vagus nerve. Chest breathing, by contrast, activates primarily the upper lungs, which have fewer of these connections. The difference in vagal tone over time is significant.
5. Alternate Nostril Breathing
7 minutes
Nadi Shodhana pranayama
A foundational yoga practice with growing clinical evidence. A 2013 study found it reduced systolic blood pressure by an average of 10 points in 6 weeks. It also improves cognitive performance, possibly by balancing activity between the brain's hemispheres.
  • Close your right nostril with your right thumb. Inhale through the left for 4 counts.
  • Close both nostrils. Hold for 2 counts.
  • Release the right nostril. Exhale for 4 counts.
  • Inhale through the right for 4 counts. Close both, hold 2. Exhale through the left for 4.
  • This is one cycle. Do 5–7 minutes, always switching after the exhale.
Left nostril breathing is associated with right brain activation and parasympathetic dominance. Right nostril breathing activates the left brain and sympathetic system. Alternating creates a balanced state that is both calm and cognitively clear.

Which one should you start with?

Start with the physiological sigh. It takes 30 seconds, requires no setup, and produces noticeable results from the first breath. Once you're convinced breathing actually works, build from there.

For a daily practice: diaphragmatic breathing for 5–10 minutes is the highest-leverage long-term investment. For acute stress moments: physiological sigh or box breathing. For sleep: 4-7-8. For general balance: alternate nostril breathing.
